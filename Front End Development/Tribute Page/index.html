Richard Matthew Stallman

Founder of the GNU Project

This is a tribute to Richard Matthew Stallman.

A timeline of Richard's life:

  • 1953: - Born to Alice Lippman, a school teacher, and Daniel Stallman, a printing press broker, in New York City.
  • 1970: - Hired for the summer following his senior year of high school to write a numerical analysis program in Fortran. Enrolled at Harvard University the following Autumn, and was known for his strong performance in the infamous freshman Math 55 course.
  • 1971: - Starts working as a porgrammer at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology Artifical Intelligence Lab.
  • 1974: - Graduates from Harvard with a Bachelor of Arts in Physics.
  • 1976: - Writes the first Emacs text editor while working at the MIT AI Lab.
  • 1983: - Announces the project to develop the GNU operating system, a Unix-like operating system meant to be entirely free software.
  • 1984: - Resigns from his position at MIT in order to focus on the GNU project full time.
  • 1985: - Starts the Free Software Foundation, a nonprofit with a worldwide mission to promote computer user freedom. GNU Emacs is widely distributed for the first time.
  • 1986: - Writes the GNU Debugger (GDB), a portable debugger that works for many programming languages.
  • 1987: - The GNU Compiler Collection (GCC) is released, available by FTP from MIT.
  • 1989: - Writes the first version of the GNU General Public License (GPL), a widely used free software license which guarantees end users the freedom to run, study, share and modify software.
  • 1990: - The GNU system is completed sans kernel, but with the GNU Hurd kernel project underway.
  • 1992: - The almost-complete GNU System is combined with the Linux kernel, which results in a complete operating system: GNU/Linux. Development on GNU Hurd slows down.
  • Current: - Has become one of the most famous software freedom activists to date, spending most of his time campaigning against software patents, digital rights management, and other legal and technical systems which take away users' freedoms.
